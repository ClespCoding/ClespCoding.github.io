Cnut the Great: How a Danish Prince United the North Sea

Posted on March 15, 2025 | By George Astin

In the early 11th century, a remarkable political entity emerged around the North Sea—a maritime empire that united England, Denmark, and Norway under a single ruler. This North Sea Empire, as historians later called it, was the creation of Cnut the Great, a Danish prince who would become one of the most powerful rulers in medieval Europe.

Born around 990 CE to the Danish king Sweyn Forkbeard, Cnut was part of a dynasty that had been harassing the coasts of England for decades. The Anglo-Saxon kingdom, ruled by Æthelred the Unready, had been paying enormous sums (Danegeld) to keep Viking raiders at bay, but this strategy ultimately failed.

The Hanseatic League: Medieval Trade Network that Shaped Northern Europe

Posted on February 27, 2025 | By George Astin

Long after the North Sea Empire had dissolved, a different kind of power emerged across the Baltic and North Seas—not a political empire, but a commercial one. The Hanseatic League, a network of merchant guilds and market towns, dominated trade in northern Europe from the 13th to the 17th century.

Beginning in the German city of Lübeck, the Hansa (as it was commonly known) expanded to include nearly 200 settlements stretching from London to Novgorod. These cities weren't united by a common government but by shared commercial interests and a desire for mutual protection in the dangerous waters of medieval trade.

Anglo-Saxon England: The Foundation of the North Sea World

Posted on January 19, 2025 | By George Astin

Before Cnut's conquest, before the Viking raids, the land that would become England was transformed by waves of Germanic migrants. The Anglo-Saxons—composed of Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and Frisians—arrived from continental Europe in the 5th century, establishing kingdoms that would eventually unite into England.

These seafaring peoples maintained connections across the North Sea, trading goods and ideas with their continental cousins. The early medieval North Sea was not a barrier but a highway, connecting rather than dividing the peoples around its shores.
